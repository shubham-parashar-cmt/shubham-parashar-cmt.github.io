Gapping a Weyl semimetal

A cleaned course-note digest on anomaly constraints, translation symmetry, interacting gap formation, and three-dimensional fractional Hall response in magnetic Weyl semimetals.

This page is a condensed public-facing version of an older course paper. It is presented as background and pedagogy, not as a new research claim.

Core question

A magnetic Weyl semimetal with two Weyl nodes at momenta k_z = ±Q has bulk gaplessness protected not by an ordinary on-site symmetry alone, but by a combination of topology, translation, and anomaly data. The natural question is:

Can strong interactions gap the Weyl fermions while preserving charge conservation, translation symmetry, and the topological response associated with the Weyl-node separation?

The conservative answer is that a symmetric interacting gap is strongly constrained. In the construction reviewed here, it is possible only at special commensurate node separations, and the resulting phase is not a trivial insulator: it has intrinsic topological order.

Anomaly and Hall-response data

The separation between Weyl nodes controls anomalous response. In a continuum description, one can encode the obstruction by coupling the system to a background electromagnetic field and to a background field for lattice translation along the node-separation direction. Equivalently, one may view the anomalous Hall conductivity as proportional to the Weyl-node separation.

A useful physical picture is that tuning 2Q interpolates between different integer Hall responses through an intervening Weyl semimetal. Therefore an interaction-driven gap, if allowed, must preserve the response data rather than simply erase the Weyl points as if they were accidental band crossings.

Warm-up: gapping a two-dimensional Dirac point

The two-dimensional Dirac problem is a useful analogy. A Dirac cone at a plateau transition carries parity-anomaly data. A naive pairing construction introduces vortices carrying nontrivial charge and statistics; the smallest condensable vortex may be a multiple vortex rather than the elementary one. The resulting symmetric gapped state is then naturally described using fractionalized charge and neutral sectors.

This is the right lesson for the Weyl problem: interactions may gap a system without producing a featureless band insulator, but only by leaving behind topological order that accounts for the anomaly.

Three-dimensional Weyl case

For a pair of Weyl nodes, ordinary pairing terms either fail to gap the spectrum in the required way or break translation symmetry. Charge-density-wave-type terms can gap opposite chiralities, but the order parameter carries momentum tied to the node separation. Translation can be preserved only at special commensurate values, such as 2Q = π in lattice units.

Vortex-loop condensation gives another way to see the obstruction. In three dimensions, vortices are loops, and their linking with lattice dislocations diagnoses how translation symmetry and Hall response are encoded. Elementary vortex loops can carry nontrivial loop statistics; a higher-multiple condensation can be required to obtain a symmetric gapped phase.

Resulting phase

At the commensurate point, the symmetric gapped phase can be understood as a three-dimensional analogue of a fractional Hall state. A parton description schematically fractionalizes the electron into charged and neutral degrees of freedom with an emergent discrete gauge structure, commonly described in this context as Z₄ topological order.

The point of the construction is not merely to remove Weyl nodes. The gapped phase must match the electrical and thermal response data that were previously carried by the gapless Weyl fermions and boundary Fermi arcs.

Safe takeaway

  • Generic magnetic Weyl semimetals cannot be symmetrically gapped by interactions without confronting anomaly and translation constraints.
  • At special commensurate node separations, an interaction-enabled symmetric gap can be possible.
  • The resulting state is not a trivial insulator; it carries intrinsic topological order and fractional Hall-type response.
  • This note should be read as a cleaned pedagogical summary of the anomaly-matching logic, not as an independent new construction.
