Working note

Fermi-liquid instabilities, van Hove scaling, and nematic quantum criticality

A cleaned background note on weak-coupling RG near Fermi surfaces, van Hove saddle points, Pomeranchuk/nematic order, and fermions coupled to soft nematic modes.

Status: public-safe pedagogical digest. The original scratch questions, unfinished calculations, and speculative research directions are intentionally omitted.

Purpose and cleanup verdict

The original note mixed a useful review of Fermi-liquid instabilities with private scratch calculations and open-ended research questions. This public version keeps the background structure: how marginal interactions arise near a Fermi surface, why van Hove points change the scaling problem, and how nematic order produces soft collective modes that can invalidate ordinary Landau quasiparticle phenomenology.

The safe public statement is limited: van Hove singularities and nematic quantum criticality provide controlled mechanisms by which Fermi-liquid behavior can become fragile. This note does not claim a complete theory of the cuprate normal state, an all-orders RG result, or a new marginal-Fermi-liquid fixed point.

Fermi liquids and the meaning of stability

A Landau Fermi liquid is organized around long-lived quasiparticles near a Fermi surface. Locally, one writes

\[ G(\omega,k) \simeq \frac{Z}{\omega - v_F k_\perp + i0^+}, \]

where \(k_\perp\) measures distance normal to the Fermi surface. The quasiparticle decay rate is parametrically small, usually \(1/\tau \sim \varepsilon^2\) in a clean Fermi liquid at low energy.

Landau's energy functional expands the energy in deformations of the distribution function,

\[ \delta E = \sum_{p} \epsilon_p\,\delta n_p + \frac12\sum_{p,p'} f_{p p'}\,\delta n_p\delta n_{p'} + \cdots . \]

The interaction function can be decomposed into angular momentum channels. A sufficiently attractive Landau parameter in a nonzero angular momentum channel triggers a Pomeranchuk instability: the Fermi surface itself becomes the order parameter.

Weak-coupling RG: three lessons

One dimension

In a one-dimensional spinless fermion problem, the low-energy theory is built from left- and right-moving modes near the Fermi points. Four-fermion interactions are marginal at tree level. At weak coupling, different one-loop channels can cancel in special cases, giving a stable gapless phase over a finite region of parameter space. Strong coupling requires methods beyond naive perturbation theory, such as bosonization or exact solutions where available.

A generic higher-dimensional Fermi surface

For a smooth Fermi surface in two or more dimensions, not every four-fermion process remains important in the infrared. Kinematics selects special channels: forward scattering and Cooper/BCS scattering. In the BCS channel, repulsive interactions are marginally irrelevant, while attractive interactions are marginally relevant and drive pairing.

A van Hove point

A square-lattice dispersion such as

\[ \epsilon(p)=-t\bigl[\cos(p_x a)+\cos(p_y a)\bigr] \]

has saddle points at momenta such as \((\pi,0)\) and \((0,\pi)\). Near a saddle point,

\[ \epsilon(p) \simeq -t(p_x^2-p_y^2). \]

The dynamical exponent is then \(z=2\), and a local four-fermion interaction is marginal by power counting. This is qualitatively different from a generic smooth Fermi-surface patch, because the density of states and available phase space are enhanced near the saddle.

The useful public lesson is that saddle-point kinematics can generate logarithmic structures and marginal-looking decay rates. The detailed channel analysis is regulator- and patch-dependent and should not be advertised as a complete fixed-point theory without a full RG treatment.

Charge nematic order as a Fermi-surface distortion

In two dimensions, a charge nematic order parameter can be represented by a symmetric traceless tensor,

\[ Q_{ij} \sim \begin{pmatrix} Q_{xx} & Q_{xy}\\ Q_{xy} & -Q_{xx} \end{pmatrix} . \]

Equivalently, the complex quadrupolar bilinear

\[ Q_2 \sim \left\langle \psi^\dagger(\partial_x+i\partial_y)^2\psi \right\rangle = |Q_2|e^{2i\theta} \]

transforms in the angular momentum \(l=2\) channel. A nonzero value distorts the Fermi surface from a circle into an ellipse and breaks rotational symmetry while preserving translation symmetry.

A schematic quadrupolar interaction has the form

\[ H = \int d^d r\; \psi^\dagger\epsilon(-i\nabla)\psi + \int d^d r d^d r'\; F(r-r')\,\mathrm{Tr}\,Q(r)Q(r'). \]

When the corresponding Landau parameter crosses its Pomeranchuk threshold, the isotropic Fermi liquid becomes unstable to a nematic metal.

Spin-orbit Pomeranchuk order

In spinful systems, one can form order parameters carrying both spin and orbital indices. A representative \(l=1\) order parameter is

\[ n^{\mu a} \sim |f_1^a|\int d^d k\; \langle \psi_\alpha^\dagger \sigma^\mu_{\alpha\beta} k^a \psi_\beta\rangle, \]

where \(\mu\) labels spin space and \(a\) labels orbital space. A symmetry-respecting Landau free energy can be organized as

\[ F[n] = r\,\mathrm{Tr}(n^Tn) + u\,\mathrm{Tr}\bigl[(n^Tn)^2\bigr] + v\,[\mathrm{Tr}(n^Tn)]^2 + \cdots . \]

Depending on the sign of quartic coefficients, different ordered phases are possible. A rank-one configuration resembles \(n^{\mu a}\sim \hat d^\mu \hat e^a\), while an isotropic spin-orbit locked configuration resembles \(n^{\mu a}\sim D_{\mu a}\) with \(D\in SO(3)\).

Nematic quantum criticality and fermion self-energies

A standard route to the critical theory is to decouple the four-fermion interaction with a Hubbard-Stratonovich field and integrate out high-energy fermions. The quadratic kernel for nematic fluctuations has the schematic form

\[ S_2[n] = \sum_{q,\omega} n_{\mu a}(q,\omega)\, \mathcal K_{\mu a,\nu b}(q,\\omega)\, n_{\nu b}(-q,-\omega), \]

where \(\mathcal K\) contains the inverse Landau interaction and a fermion bubble. Near the critical point, soft bosonic modes couple back to the fermions. This feedback is the origin of non-Fermi-liquid behavior in nematic metals.

In three-dimensional versions of the problem, one often finds overdamped transverse collective modes with a dynamical exponent near \(z=3\) and less singular fermion self-energies than in two dimensions. In two dimensions, patch theories of a Fermi surface coupled to a Landau-damped boson famously produce stronger non-Fermi-liquid behavior, such as self-energies scaling schematically like \(\omega^{2/3}\), with additional subtleties beyond low-order perturbation theory.

The robust lesson is not a single exponent quoted out of context. It is the mechanism: near a nematic quantum critical point, the soft deformation mode of the Fermi surface remains gapless and strongly scatters low-energy fermions.

What this note deliberately does not claim

  • It does not claim that a van Hove singularity alone proves a marginal-Fermi-liquid phase.
  • It does not claim a complete explanation of the strange-metal normal state of doped cuprates.
  • It does not include private scratch calculations, channel-by-channel contour tricks, or unfinished RG questions.
  • It should be read as a cleaned conceptual map connecting Fermi-liquid stability, saddle-point scaling, and nematic criticality.
