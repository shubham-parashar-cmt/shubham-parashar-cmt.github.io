Working note

A clean route to zeta regularization

Heat kernels, Mellin transforms, finite parts, and meromorphic continuation.

PDF TeX source Back to notes

Purpose

This note separates three operations that are often conflated in informal discussions of zeta regularization: ordinary summation, regulator-dependent finite parts, and analytic continuation. The basic example is the exponential cutoff of $\sum_{n\ge 1}n$, followed by the Mellin-transform construction of the Riemann zeta function and its spectral generalization for Laplace-type operators.

The statement $1+2+3+\cdots=-1/12$ is not an ordinary summation statement. A careful statement is that the exponential-cutoff finite part is $-1/12$, and the analytically continued Riemann zeta function satisfies $\zeta(-1)=-1/12$.

Three notions

  • Ordinary sum: $\sum_n a_n$ means the limit of partial sums, when that limit exists.
  • Regularization: replace a divergent expression by a convergent family $F(\varepsilon)$ and study $\varepsilon\to0^+$.
  • Analytic continuation: extend a holomorphic function from its original domain to a larger domain, usually meromorphically.

A convergent cutoff calculation

For $\varepsilon>0$, define the honest convergent sum

$$ S(\varepsilon)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n e^{-\varepsilon n}. $$

With $q=e^{-\varepsilon}$,

$$ \sum_{n\ge1}nq^n=\frac{q}{(1-q)^2}, \qquad S(\varepsilon)=\frac{e^{-\varepsilon}}{(1-e^{-\varepsilon})^2}. $$

The Bernoulli expansion gives

$$ S(\varepsilon)=\frac{1}{\varepsilon^2}-\frac{1}{12}+O(\varepsilon^2), \qquad \varepsilon\to0^+. $$

Thus the divergent piece is $\varepsilon^{-2}$, while the cutoff-independent constant term is $-1/12$. In this scheme,

$$ \sum_{n\ge1}^{(\mathrm{FP})} n := \operatorname{FP}_{\varepsilon\to0^+}\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n e^{-\varepsilon n} = -\frac{1}{12}. $$

Mellin transforms

For a function $F(t)$ on $(0,\infty)$, the Mellin transform is

$$ \mathcal{M}[F](s)=\int_0^{\infty}t^{s-1}F(t)\,\mathrm{d}t. $$

If $F(t)\sim\sum_j c_jt^{\alpha_j}$ as $t\to0^+$, then the terms $c_jt^{\alpha_j}$ generate possible poles at $s=-\alpha_j$. The continuation mechanism is to subtract finitely many small-$t$ singular terms, integrate the subtracted expression in a larger strip, and add the subtracted terms back as explicit meromorphic functions of $s$.

Riemann zeta

For $\operatorname{Re}(s)>1$,

$$ \zeta(s)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n^{-s} =\frac{1}{\Gamma(s)}\int_0^{\infty}\frac{t^{s-1}}{e^t-1}\,\mathrm{d}t. $$

Near $t=0$,

$$ \frac{1}{e^t-1}=\frac{1}{t}-\frac{1}{2}+\frac{t}{12}-\frac{t^3}{720}+\cdots. $$

Subtracting and adding back these terms produces the meromorphic continuation of $\zeta(s)$. In that analytically continued sense,

$$ \zeta(-1)=-\frac{1}{12}. $$

Spectral zeta functions

Let $M$ be a compact Riemannian manifold and let $\Delta\ge0$ be the Laplace-Beltrami operator, with discrete spectrum $0=\lambda_0\le\lambda_1\le\cdots$ and $\lambda_k\to\infty$. The heat trace is

$$ \Theta(t)=\operatorname{Tr}(e^{-t\Delta})=\sum_{k\ge0}e^{-t\lambda_k},\qquad t>0. $$

As $t\to0^+$,

$$ \Theta(t)\sim (4\pi t)^{-\dim M/2}\sum_{j\ge0}a_jt^{j/2}. $$

The spectral zeta function is initially defined for $\operatorname{Re}(s)$ large by

$$ \zeta_\Delta(s)=\sum_{k\ge1}\lambda_k^{-s}, $$

and is related to the heat trace by

$$ \zeta_\Delta(s)=\frac{1}{\Gamma(s)}\int_0^{\infty}t^{s-1}\bigl(\Theta(t)-1\bigr)\,\mathrm{d}t. $$

The heat-kernel coefficients $a_j$ control the possible pole structure of the meromorphic continuation.
