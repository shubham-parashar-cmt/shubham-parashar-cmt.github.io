Public-safe working note

Worldline EFT, solitons, and Q-clouds

A conservative synthesis of background material on compact-object effective field theory, tidal response, and synchronized scalar clouds around Kerr black holes.

Status: abbreviated public version. Private model-building steps, implementation details, and speculative claims are intentionally omitted.

Purpose

This note keeps only the reusable, non-proprietary background from a longer private set of notes. The organizing question is how one should describe an extended compact object - such as a black hole, neutron star, Q-ball, or scalar condensate - when it is probed at length scales much larger than its internal size.

Scope guard. This is not a public proposal for a new hairy-black-hole mechanism. It is a cleaned conceptual map: scale separation, worldline operators, matching, Love-number language, and the standard synchronized-cloud setup.

Scale separation

The compact-binary problem is naturally organized into three regions. The internal zone resolves the compact object itself, with characteristic radius \(r_s\). The near zone resolves the orbital separation \(r\). The radiation zone resolves gravitational waves with wavelength of order \(r/v\), where \(v\) is the orbital velocity. The useful hierarchy is therefore schematically

\[ r_s \ll r \ll \lambda_{\rm rad} . \]

Effective field theory uses this hierarchy by integrating out the short-distance degrees of freedom and replacing the compact object by a worldline decorated with all local operators allowed by symmetry. The coefficients of those operators encode the unresolved internal physics.

Worldline EFT skeleton

At distances large compared with \(r_s\), the leading term is the point-particle action

\[ S_{\rm pp} = -m \int \dd\tau . \]

Finite-size effects are added as a derivative expansion along the worldline:

\[ S_{\rm eff} = -m\int \dd\tau + \sum_i C_i \int \dd\tau\, \mathcal O_i[g_{\mu\nu},u^\mu,\nabla_\alpha,\ldots] . \]

The Wilson coefficients \(C_i\) are fixed by matching: compute an observable in the full theory and in the EFT, expand both at low momentum, and choose \(C_i\) so the two descriptions agree to the desired order in \(k r_s\).

Tidal response and Love-number language

For a nonspinning compact object, the first physically important finite-size operators are usually written in terms of the electric and magnetic parts of the Weyl tensor,

\[ E_{\mu\nu}=C_{\mu\alpha\nu\beta}u^\alpha u^\beta, \qquad B_{\mu\nu}= {}^\star C_{\mu\alpha\nu\beta}u^\alpha u^\beta . \]

A minimal tidal-response action has the schematic form

\[ S_{\rm tidal} = \frac12 \int \dd\tau \left[ C_E E_{\mu\nu}E^{\mu\nu} + C_B B_{\mu\nu}B^{\mu\nu} + \cdots \right] . \]

The coefficients \(C_E\) and \(C_B\) are the EFT representatives of static tidal response, often discussed in terms of Love numbers. The same logic extends to higher multipoles and to frequency-dependent response functions.

For this public note, the important point is methodological: Love numbers are not decorations added by hand; they are matching coefficients in a long-distance effective action.

Potential modes and post-Newtonian organization

In the near zone, off-shell gravitational potential modes mediate conservative binding. They are integrated out to produce an effective action for the worldlines. Radiation modes remain as long-distance degrees of freedom and couple to multipole moments of the binary.

A useful bookkeeping device is the temporal Kaluza-Klein, or NRG, decomposition of the metric:

\[ \dd s^2 = e^{2\phi}(\dd t-A_i\dd x^i)^2 - e^{-2\phi/(d-2)}\gamma_{ij}\dd x^i\dd x^j . \]

This separates a scalar gravitational potential \(\phi\), a gravimagnetic vector \(A_i\), and a spatial metric \(\gamma_{ij}\). It is a convenient parametrization for post-Newtonian EFT calculations because different fields have transparent velocity scaling.

Synchronized scalar clouds on Kerr

The scalar-cloud side of the problem can be stated without committing to any private model-building claim. Consider a complex scalar field with a Q-ball-type self-interaction on a fixed Kerr background. A standard synchronized ansatz is

\[ \Phi(t,r,\theta,\varphi) = e^{-i\omega t+i m\varphi}\Psi(r,\theta), \qquad \omega=m\Omega_H, \]

where \(\Omega_H\) is the horizon angular velocity. The synchronization condition makes the field stationary in the co-rotating frame and removes net scalar flux through the horizon. Substituting the ansatz into the scalar equation gives a nonlinear elliptic boundary-value problem for \(\Psi(r,\theta)\), with regularity at the horizon and axis and decay at infinity.

This public note stops at the level of the standard setup. Numerical implementation choices, matching strategies, and any proposed mechanism connecting clouds to worldline coefficients are deliberately not included here.
